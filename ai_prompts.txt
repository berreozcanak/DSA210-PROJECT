Answer1:
For your project, since you are an **Industrial Engineering** student and focusing on "efficiency" (focus and productivity), you can use these EDA techniques that are both useful for your analysis and consistent with what was taught in your recitations:

### 1. Data Distribution (Histograms)
**Why:** Before you do any t-tests, you should check if your Focus and Productivity scores are "normally distributed" (the bell curve). 
*   **Technique:** Use `sns.histplot(df['focus'], kde=True)`.
*   **IE Insight:** Most IE models assume things are normal. If your data is heavily skewed (most people are very distracted or very focused), it changes how you interpret your results.

### 2. Grouped Averages (The `.groupby` method)
**Why:** In Recitation 2, you used `.groupby` to find average points by position in the NBA. You can do the exact same thing for your study locations.
*   **Technique:** `df.groupby('location')[['focus', 'productivity']].mean()`
*   **IE Insight:** This gives you a quick "Leaderboard" of locations. Is the Library actually better than a Cafe, or is it just a myth?

### 3. Impact of Hue (Boxplots with a second variable)
**Why:** This is a bit more "advanced" but very common in your recitations. You can see how one variable affects focus, split by another.
*   **Technique:** `sns.boxplot(x="location", y="focus", hue="audio", data=df)`
*   **Insight:** This answers: "Does music help in a Cafe but hurt in the Library?" It shows the *interaction* between noise and music.

### 4. Correlation Analysis (Scatter Plots)
**Why:** You have numerical data like Sleep Hours and Stress. It's a classic IE question: "Does less sleep lead to higher stress?"
*   **Technique:** `sns.scatterplot(x='sleep', y='stress', data=df)`
*   **Insight:** If you see a line forming, you know there is a strong relationship. You can also add `sns.regplot` to draw a line through the dots like in Recitation 2.

### 5. Categorical Comparisons (Bar Charts)
**Why:** To see the "profile" of your survey takers.
*   **Technique:** `df['department'].value_counts().plot(kind='bar')`
*   **Insight:** Helpful for your "Data Characteristics" section of the report to show which departments participated the most.
